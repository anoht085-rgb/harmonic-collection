Harmonic Collection — Layers

1970s — Day to Night

Four temporal layers of style: Boho · Minimalist · Romantic · Punk.

Layer I

Boho Morning

Early in the decade, the handmade textures of late-’60s hippie style softened into something airy and romantic. Crochet, gauze, and patchwork drifted from counterculture into high fashion, with Zandra Rhodes, Ossie Clark, Laura Ashley, Gunne Sax, and Giorgio di Sant’Angelo turning free-spirited craft into floating dresses and languid separates. As Vogue noted in 1970, “languorously falling things” invited a more introspective kind of glamour—less protest, more poetry.

Layer II

Minimalist Afternoon

Polyester, the Fabric of a 1970s Life. The decade wrapped itself in easy-care synthetics—especially double-knit polyester— which gave sleek suits, pointed collars, and clingy separates their modern line. As couture waned (Balenciaga famously closed his house in 1968), ready-to-wear surged: designers like Halston and Norma Kamali cut streamlined pieces that traveled, moved, and never begged for an iron. Polyester’s wrinkle-resistance and low-maintenance allure felt liberating for women stepping into new professional spaces. By decade’s end, the synthetic sheen lost some charm, but its imprint on 1970s minimalism—efficiency, fluidity, and everyday polish—was indelible.

Layer III

Romantic Night

Picturing femininity. In the 1970s, fashion photography pressed at the edges of taste and taboo, mirroring shifting views on sexuality, power, religion, and even violence. Helmut Newton staged charged scenes of poised women whose confidence felt both glamorous and unsettling; Guy Bourdin and Gian Paolo Barbieri pursued darker, character-driven narratives that often eclipsed the clothes. At the same time, the idea of who could embody beauty widened: black and androgynous models appeared more regularly in mainstream magazines. Deborah Turbeville and Sarah Moon—both former models—offered contemplative, distinctly female perspectives, reframing desire and objectification with dreamlike remove.

Layer IV

Punk Afterparty

Punk ripped through the late ’70s with Westwood and McLaren’s insurgent graphics, torn tartans, and hardware-as-attitude. It wasn’t polish; it was provocation—street-level styling turned into an oppositional uniform that rewired fashion from the outside in.
